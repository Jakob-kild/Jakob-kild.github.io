Chapter 1

The city-level view

The first question is simple: how many incidents are recorded each year? This static Matplotlib figure summarizes the full dataset at the broadest level. It is the most compact view on the page, but also the one that sets the stakes for everything that follows.

  • The chart shows complete-year incident totals for San Francisco.
  • It establishes long-run variation before we zoom into specific types or moments of the day.
  • It works as a baseline: later charts explain how that overall volume is distributed.
The yearly view answers how much. The rest of the page explains when and where those incidents take shape.

Static Matplotlib export from the course notebook: a broad overview of incident volume across complete years.

Chapter 2

Crime has a daily rhythm

Totals flatten the lived texture of the city. The animated Plotly chart brings that texture back by comparing normalized hourly distributions for selected focus crimes. Each year becomes a frame, and each crime type leaves behind a different shape over the 24-hour day.

  • Because the counts are normalized, the chart emphasizes shape rather than raw volume.
  • This makes it easier to compare behavioral timing across crime categories.
  • The animation also reveals whether those hourly patterns remain stable or drift from year to year.

In other words, the question changes from “How much crime is there?” to “What kinds of activity cluster at what time?”

Chapter 3

Patterns become places

Temporal structure is only part of the story. The Folium heatmap translates the dataset into urban space, showing where incidents concentrate across San Francisco. Compared with district-level choropleths, this view feels more immediate because it is built directly from raw latitude and longitude coordinates.

  • The heatmap highlights concentration rather than administrative boundaries.
  • Pan and zoom reveal how intensity changes at different map scales.
  • Together with the yearly and hourly views, it connects aggregate patterns to real neighborhoods and corridors.
Once the map appears, the data stops feeling abstract. The city becomes visible as a set of hotspots, gaps, and repeated spatial routines.

What the three views say together

A compact reading of the data

  • Across years: incident volume changes over time, giving the dataset a long-run structure.
  • Across hours: crime categories do not follow one universal daily schedule; their peaks and lulls differ.
  • Across space: incidents are unevenly distributed, clustering in specific parts of the city.

The storytelling goal is not just to host three visualizations, but to let each one answer a different layer of the same question.
