San Francisco police incident data, 2003–2025

COVID impact on San Francisco crime rates

The pandemic did not move every crime category in the same direction. Some recorded crimes rose during the COVID period, while others fell, revealing a city whose patterns shifted both by category and by place.

Three visualizations Author-led narrative Dark mode design
Introduction

What this data can show — and what it cannot

This story uses San Francisco police incident records from the course dataset. These records reflect incidents that were reported, observed, and logged by the police. That means they are useful for studying changes in recorded crime patterns, but they do not measure the total amount of harm or lawbreaking in the city directly.

That caution matters here. A rise in one category does not prove that behavior itself rose for only one reason, and a decline does not necessarily mean the underlying problem disappeared. Still, the dataset clearly shows that the COVID period was not a uniform shock. Different categories moved in different directions.

Figure 1

Not all crime categories changed the same way during COVID

The first chart sets up the main story. During the COVID period, recorded burglary and motor vehicle theft rose relative to their immediately preceding levels, while assault moved downward. Robbery followed a different path again.

The point is not that “crime went up” or “crime went down” in one simple way. The point is that the pandemic period appears to have reshaped the city’s crime mix. Any broad claim about total crime hides that divergence.

Interpretation

A pandemic shock can change incentives, movement, and opportunity

One plausible interpretation is that the COVID period changed everyday routines across the city: where people traveled, how businesses operated, which spaces were left empty, and where economic stress became most visible. Those shifts can change the opportunity structure for different kinds of incidents.

But the safest conclusion from this dataset is narrower. The records show that burglary and motor vehicle theft were more resilient — or even elevated — during the COVID period compared with some other categories. That is a statement about recorded patterns, not a complete explanation of cause.

Figure 2

The burglary pattern also spread geographically

The second visualization narrows the focus to burglary. Before COVID, recorded burglary incidents were concentrated in a more limited set of hotspots. During COVID, that concentration appears more spatially diffuse.

The map suggests that the pandemic-era shift was not only about more incidents in the same places. It also changed where burglary pressure appeared across San Francisco.

Comparison

Place mattered just as much as category

Once the city is broken into police districts, the COVID effect looks uneven. Some districts saw much larger changes than others. That means the pandemic-era shift was not only categorical and not only citywide. It was spatially uneven too.

This is where an interactive chart helps. Rather than asking the reader to decode every district from a static figure, the last visualization lets you switch categories and inspect how before-COVID and during-COVID averages compare district by district.

Figure 3

District-level numbers confirm the uneven shift

The final chart moves from pattern to comparison. It shows average yearly incidents in each police district before COVID and during COVID. The selector lets you switch between burglary, motor vehicle theft, robbery, and assault.

Together with the first two figures, this makes the broader point concrete: COVID did not affect all recorded crimes equally, and it did not affect all parts of the city equally either.

Conclusion

The main story is divergence

The clearest takeaway is not that COVID made crime rise or fall across the board. It is that the period introduced divergence. Burglary and motor vehicle theft rose, assault fell, and burglary’s geographic footprint appears to have widened.

That makes this a story about disruption rather than a single trend line. The pandemic did not push every crime category in the same direction, and it did not affect every part of San Francisco in the same way.
