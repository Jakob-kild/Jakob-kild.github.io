COVID impact on San Francisco crime rates

The pandemic did not reshape all recorded crimes in the same way. Some categories, like burglary and motor vehicle theft, rose during the COVID period, while others, like assault, fell. The result was not one simple citywide trend, but a shift in both category mix and geography.

Introduction

Reading police incident data with care

This story uses San Francisco police incident records from the course dataset. These records describe incidents that were reported, observed, and logged by police. That makes them useful for studying changes in recorded crime patterns, but not for making simple claims about the total amount of crime or harm in the city.

That distinction matters for the pandemic years. A rise in one category does not, by itself, prove one cause, and a drop in another does not mean the underlying problem disappeared. Still, the data clearly shows that COVID did not affect all recorded crime categories equally.

Figure 1

Not all categories moved in the same direction during COVID

The first figure establishes the central claim. During the COVID period, burglary and motor vehicle theft rose relative to the immediately preceding years, while assault declined. Robbery followed a different path again.

This means that any single statement about “crime going up” or “crime going down” hides an important pattern. The pandemic period appears to have changed the composition of recorded crime, not just the overall total.

Interpretation

A disruption to routine life can reshape opportunity

One plausible interpretation is that the pandemic changed everyday urban life: movement patterns, business activity, empty streets, closed workplaces, and household stress. Those disruptions may have changed the opportunity structure for different types of incidents.

But the safest claim stays close to the data. What the records show is that the COVID period coincided with a divergence across crime categories. Some categories rose, some fell, and that divergence is the main pattern this story follows.

Figure 2

Burglary also became less geographically concentrated

The second visualization narrows the story to burglary. Before COVID, burglary was more tightly concentrated in a limited set of hotspots. During COVID, that concentration appears to spread across a broader part of the city.

This suggests that the pandemic-era change was not only a matter of more incidents in the same places. It also changed where burglary was recorded across San Francisco.

Comparison

The shift was uneven across districts too

Category-level change is only part of the picture. Once the city is broken into police districts, the COVID period looks uneven there as well. Some districts changed much more than others.

That makes the pandemic-era shift both categorical and spatial. The final chart lets the reader compare district-level averages before and during COVID for several categories directly.

Figure 3

District-level comparisons make the unevenness visible

The final visualization compares average yearly incidents in each police district before COVID and during COVID. The selector lets you switch between burglary, motor vehicle theft, robbery, and assault.

Together, the three figures tell one story: COVID did not push all recorded crime in one direction. It changed the balance among categories, widened burglary’s spatial footprint, and affected districts unevenly.

Conclusion

The main story is divergence, not one overall trend

The clearest takeaway is not that COVID simply increased or decreased crime in San Francisco. It is that the pandemic period produced divergence. Burglary and motor vehicle theft rose, assault fell, and burglary’s geography appears to have widened.

This makes the COVID period look less like one clean trend line and more like a reshaping of the city’s recorded crime profile. That is a more careful and more honest conclusion than treating all categories alike.
